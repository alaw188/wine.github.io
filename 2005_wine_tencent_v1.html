A Tasting of Five 2005 Red Burgundies

A Vintage Dinner

Five bottles from one of Burgundy's modern legends — three from Méo-Camuzet, a Corton Grand Cru from Montille, and a Gouges from Nuits-St-Georges. Two decades in, the 2005s are arriving at their plateau: structured, perfumed, and built to reward a thoughtful pour.

How to handle them

Serving & Decanting

At ~20 years old these are mature but still carry the tannic grip and extract of a great structured vintage. The goal is to coax aromas open without bruising the delicate Pinot fruit.

1

Decant, gently

Pour into a clean decanter to separate the light sediment that has formed and to let the wine breathe. Keep it gentle — avoid violent splashing. A short aeration of 30–90 min is plenty; Pinot is fragile and oxidises quickly.

2

Serve cool

Aim for 15–16 °C (59–61 °F). Too warm and the alcohol dominates; too cold and the bouquet shuts down. Slightly cool is safer — the wine warms gracefully in the glass.

3

Taste as you go

Open an hour ahead, then sample a small glass. If a wine smells tired or hollow, stop the aeration. If it's still tight, give it more air. Every bottle is individual at this age.

Preparation checklist

  • 24 h before: stand bottles upright so sediment settles to the base.
  • Decant carefully: pour in one steady stream; leave the last drop (and grit) behind.
  • Glassware: a large Burgundy balloon captures the perfume — don't skimp on bowl size.
  • Order: pour lighter & more elegant first, the biggest last (see flight below).
  • Don't over-decant: if a wine flattens, it has had enough air — stop.

Why 2005 matters

The 2005 Vintage

2005 is widely regarded as one of Burgundy's greatest modern red vintages — a year of near-perfect conditions that produced wines of remarkable depth, structure and longevity.

The growing season

A dry, warm, sunny summer with just enough water stress to concentrate the crop. The result was exceptional phenolic maturity — ripe, velvety tannins married to a bright natural acidity.

The wines

Deeply coloured, powerful and tightly wound in youth. Twenty years on, the structure is softening while the fruit and terroir speak clearly. Many will hold through the 2030s and beyond.

At the table tonight

Expect tertiary notes — forest floor, dried rose, spice, game — layered over conserved dark fruit. A careful decant helps integrate the remaining tannin and lift the aromatics.

Producer story

Domaine Méo-Camuzet of Vosne-Romanée is one of the Côte de Nuits' most celebrated names. Jean-Nicolas Méo took the reins in 1989, and the domaine's history is entwined with the legendary Henri Jayer, who farmed Méo-Camuzet's vines for decades and advised through the transition to estate bottling. The wines are refined, perfumed and built for the long haul.

Terroir & climat

Aux Boudots sits at the far north-western edge of Nuits-St-Georges, right against the Vosne-Romanée boundary (just below Les Malconsorts). That cooler, limestone-rich terroir lends a Vosne-like finesse — Boudots is often described as "Nuits dressed in Vosne clothing," carrying Nuits structure with Vosne perfume.

The 2005 character

A structured, powerful vintage that here is tamed by Boudots' elegance. Expect dark cherry and blackberry over violet and sweet spice, with fine, ripe tannins and a long, mineral finish.

Tasting notes & food

Dark berry fruit, rose, liquorice and a whisper of earth. A wonderful partner to roast duck, game birds, or an aged Comté. Decant ~45–60 min and serve in a large Burgundy glass.

Producer story

Same domaine as above — Méo-Camuzet, where Jean-Nicolas Méo channels a style informed by the Jayer legacy: aromatic, precise, and quietly powerful. In Vosne-Romanée the domaine's holdings are among the village's most admired.

Terroir & climat

Les Chaumes is a central, slightly warmer Premier Cru in Vosne-Romanée, known for producing the village's most sensual, early-charming wines — silky, floral and perfumed rather than brooding. It is the gentle counterpoint to the more muscular Nuits cuvées.

The 2005 character

Even in a powerful year, Chaumes keeps its poise: the 2005 shows a silkiness that lifts the vintage's density. Rose, violet and red cherry lead, with sandalwood and a caressing texture.

Tasting notes & food

Violets, red cherry, rose petal and a fine mineral seam. Ideal with roast pigeon, herb-crusted lamb, or a truffle dish. Gentle decanting of 30–45 min is enough to unveil the perfume.

Producer story

From the same Méo-Camuzet cellar as Boudots and Chaumes, Aux Murgers is the most serious of the domaine's three Nuits Premiers Crus in this flight — a wine that rewards patience and a generous decant.

Terroir & climat

"Murgers" is the local word for piles of limestone stones cleared from the vineyard. Aux Murgers lies in the northern Nuits sector near the Vosne boundary, on stony, well-drained limestone that yields a deeper, spicier, more ageworthy wine than Boudots.

The 2005 character

The most brooding of the Méo trio. Dark plum and black cherry, liquorice, clove and a stony earthiness frame firm, ripe tannins. This is a wine that still wants air and time — give it the longest decant of the three.

Tasting notes & food

Concentrated dark fruit, spice and forest floor with a long, structured finish. Match with braised beef, venison, or a hard mountain cheese. Decant 60–90 min; serve at ~16 °C.

Producer story

Domaine de Montille of Volnay, long led by the late Hubert de Montille, is revered for a classical, restrained house style — wines of precision and enormous longevity rather than immediate charm. Today it is run by Etienne and Alix de Montille, carrying that disciplined, terroir-first philosophy forward.

Terroir & climat

Clos du Roi ("the King's enclosure") is a historic walled parcel within Corton, on the Aloxe-Corton side of the hill at mid-slope. The limestone-rich clos gives wines of depth, power and structure — the grandest terroir of the evening.

The 2005 character

A monumental Grand Cru that will comfortably outlive the Premiers Crus around it. Blackcurrant and cassis, iron, graphite and forest floor, with tannins of real authority and a very long finish. This is the wine to pour last and linger over.

Tasting notes & food

Profound dark fruit, mineral and game notes, monumental structure. Pair with roast venison, lamb, or truffle. Decant generously (90–120 min) and serve a touch warmer, ~16–17 °C.

Producer story

Domaine Henri Gouges has been family-owned in Nuits-St-Georges since 1919. Henri Gouges was a pioneer who bottled his own wine early and helped shape Burgundy's appellation system as a founder-member of the INAO. The house style is traditionally firm and ageworthy — wines that demand patience.

Terroir & climat

Les Chaignots is the domaine's only parcel on the northern (Vosne) side of the village: a steep, south-east-facing slope of limestone scree below the Combe de la Serrée. That privileged site gives Chaignots a finesse unusual for the often-austere Gouges style.

The 2005 character

Structured and robust in the mould of the vintage, yet with the site's underlying elegance. Dark cherry and raspberry, warm spice and a mineral, firmly tannic finish that needs air to unfold.

Tasting notes & food

Dark cherry, raspberry, clove and a stony minerality. Lovely with charcuterie, roast poultry, or an aged Gruyère. Decant 60–90 min and serve at 15–16 °C.
